A filmmaker rooted in honesty

I didn't set out to become a filmmaker — storytelling found me first. Growing up, I was always the kid with a camera in hand, obsessed with the way light moved through leaves in the afternoon, the way a single expression could carry more weight than a thousand words. It was never about the technology. It was always about people, and the infinite complexity of their inner worlds.

After graduating with a Bachelor of Fine Arts in Film Production, I spent my early years on set as a camera operator, absorbing everything I could from every director, gaffer, and sound engineer I worked alongside. Those years taught me a foundational truth: the best creative work is never a solo act. It is a conversation — between the crew, the subject, and the audience who will eventually experience it.

"I believe that every frame should feel like it was made with intention — because every moment we capture is a moment someone lived."

The editor's eye

Editing was where I truly came into my own. There is something almost meditative about the process — sitting in a dark room, sifting through hours of footage, and slowly discovering the film that was hiding inside. The edit is where stories breathe. Where pacing becomes emotional architecture. Where silence can hit harder than any line of dialogue.

Over the past decade, I have edited for some of the most ambitious brands in the industry — from intimate documentary work for Chevron to high-energy commercial cuts for Lululemon. Each project arrives with its own unique heartbeat, and my job has always been to find it and amplify it, never to impose a style, but to serve the story.

I have had the privilege of working out of Creative Hub Chicago and later Paradise Studios, where I currently lead the post-production team. Leading a team has reshaped how I think about creativity — it is less about individual genius and more about creating the conditions in which everyone around you can do their best work.

Beyond the studio

When I am not in the edit suite, I am usually watching something — films from the French New Wave, a documentary someone recommended three years ago, or a short I found on a corner of the internet I have never visited before. I believe that a filmmaker who stops consuming is a filmmaker who has stopped growing.

I also teach occasional workshops on narrative editing at local film schools. There is a particular joy in watching a student discover the cut that changes everything — the moment when they understand that editing is not about removing what is bad, but about finding what is essential. I try to pass on what the best collaborators in my career once showed me: that craft, patience, and genuine curiosity are the only tools that truly matter.

If any of this resonates with you — if you have a project that needs a partner who will bring both technical precision and genuine care — I would love to hear from you.
